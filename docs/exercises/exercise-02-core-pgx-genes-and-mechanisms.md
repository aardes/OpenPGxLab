# Exercise 2: Core PGx Genes and Mechanisms

This exercise set is designed to reinforce the key ideas from **Module 2: Core PGx Genes and Mechanisms**.

## Learning Goals

By completing these exercises, the learner should be able to:

- distinguish the major classes of PGx genes
- explain the difference between enzymes, transporters, and targets
- understand why prodrugs require special interpretation
- recognize how one drug may be influenced by more than one gene
- explain the concept of phenoconversion

---

# Part 1: Quick Check Questions

## Question 1

Which of the following is the **best** description of a drug-metabolizing enzyme?

A. A protein that moves drugs across membranes  
B. A protein that chemically transforms a drug  
C. A protein that always activates the immune system  
D. A protein that stores drugs in the nucleus  

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> B. A protein that chemically transforms a drug</p>
  <p>Drug-metabolizing enzymes are involved in changing the chemical form of a drug, often affecting activation, clearance, or exposure.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 2

Which of the following is the **best** description of a drug transporter?

A. A protein that always destroys drugs  
B. A protein that changes a drug into its active metabolite  
C. A protein that helps move drugs into, out of, or between tissues  
D. A protein that measures drug concentration in blood  

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> C. A protein that helps move drugs into, out of, or between tissues</p>
  <p>Transporters influence where a drug goes in the body, how much reaches tissues, and sometimes how efficiently it is eliminated.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 3

Which of the following belongs mainly to **pharmacodynamics**?

A. Reduced clearance of an active drug  
B. Reduced uptake of a drug into a target tissue  
C. A change in the drug target that alters response  
D. Increased excretion through the kidney  

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> C. A change in the drug target that alters response</p>
  <p>Pharmacodynamics is mainly about what the drug does to the body, including target interaction and biological response.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 4

Why is a **prodrug** especially important in PGx?

A. Because prodrugs never need metabolism  
B. Because they must be activated by the body  
C. Because they are always safer than active drugs  
D. Because they are never affected by genetics  

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> B. Because they must be activated by the body</p>
  <p>If activation is reduced, the prodrug may not work well even if the drug was prescribed correctly.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 5

Which statement is most accurate?

A. One drug is always influenced by only one gene  
B. Every PGx effect is caused by a transporter  
C. One drug may be influenced by several genes  
D. Only immune-related genes matter in PGx  

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> C. One drug may be influenced by several genes</p>
  <p>A single drug may be influenced by enzymes, transporters, targets, and in some cases immune-related genes.</p>
</details>

# Part 2: True or False

## Question 6

A transporter and a metabolizing enzyme do the same job.

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> False</p>
  <p>A metabolizing enzyme changes the chemical form of a drug, while a transporter changes where the drug moves.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 7

For an active drug, reduced metabolism may increase drug exposure and toxicity risk.

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> True</p>
  <p>If the drug is cleared more slowly, it may remain in the body longer and reach higher levels.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 8

For a prodrug, reduced activation may cause reduced treatment effect.

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> True</p>
  <p>If the prodrug is not efficiently converted to the active form, the treatment may fail.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 9

Phenoconversion means that actual drug response can differ from genotype-predicted behavior because of other factors.

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> True</p>
  <p>Co-medications, disease state, inflammation, or enzyme inhibition/induction can shift functional drug metabolism away from what genotype alone predicts.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 10

Immune-related PGx is mainly about how fast a drug is metabolized.

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> False</p>
  <p>Immune-related PGx is more about abnormal immune reactions and hypersensitivity risk than drug metabolism speed.</p>
</details>

# Part 3: Short Answer Questions

## Question 11

What is the difference between a metabolizing enzyme and a transporter?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>A metabolizing enzyme chemically changes the drug, while a transporter influences where the drug moves in the body, such as into or out of tissues or cells.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 12

Why can the same principle of “slow metabolism” have different consequences for active drugs and prodrugs?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>For an active drug, slow metabolism may increase drug exposure and toxicity risk. For a prodrug, reduced metabolism or reduced activation may produce too little active drug and reduce treatment effect.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 13

Why is it not enough to think of PGx as a one-gene-one-drug problem?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>Because many drugs are influenced by multiple biological systems, such as enzymes, transporters, targets, and sometimes immune-related genes.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 14

What is phenoconversion in simple terms?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>Phenoconversion means that a person’s real-life drug metabolism or response can differ from what their genotype alone would predict, often because of other medications or clinical factors.</p>
</details>

# Part 5: Case-Based Questions

## Case 1

A patient receives an active drug. Because of reduced enzyme activity, the drug is cleared more slowly than expected.

### Question
What is the most likely consequence?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>The most likely consequence is increased drug exposure, which may raise the risk of toxicity or side effects.</p>
</details>

---

## Case 2

A patient receives a prodrug, but the treatment is ineffective. Genetic testing shows reduced activity in the enzyme responsible for activation.

### Question
What is the most likely explanation?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>The most likely explanation is reduced activation of the prodrug, resulting in too little active drug being produced.</p>
</details>

---

## Case 3

A patient has a genotype suggesting normal metabolism, but is taking another medication that strongly inhibits the relevant enzyme.

### Question
What concept does this illustrate?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>This illustrates <strong>phenoconversion</strong>.</p>
  <p>Although the genotype suggests normal function, the patient may behave more like a poor metabolizer because of the inhibitor.</p>
</details>

---

## Case 4

A drug reaches normal blood levels, but the patient’s biological response is weaker than expected.

### Question
Which type of PGx mechanism might explain this?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>A pharmacodynamic mechanism, such as a change in the drug target or response pathway, may explain the weaker response.</p>
</details>

---

## Case 5

A learner says: “If metabolism is reduced, the drug will always become stronger.”

### Question
Why is this statement incorrect?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>Because the outcome depends on whether the drug is already active or is a prodrug. For prodrugs, reduced activation may make the treatment less effective rather than stronger.</p>
</details>

# Part 7: Reflection Questions

## Question 19

Why do you think CYP genes are discussed so often in PGx?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>Because many clinically important drugs are metabolized by CYP enzymes, and genetic variation in these enzymes can strongly affect drug exposure and response.</p>
</details>

---

## Question 20

Why is phenoconversion important in clinical interpretation?

<details class="pgx-answer">
  <summary><strong>Show answer</strong></summary>
  <p>Because genotype alone may not reflect actual drug behavior in a patient if other drugs or clinical conditions change enzyme activity.</p>
</details>
