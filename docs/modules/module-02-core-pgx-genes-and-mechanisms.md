# Module 2: Core PGx Genes and Mechanisms

## At a Glance

In Module 1, we learned that genetic variation can influence drug response.

In this module, we go one level deeper and ask:

**Which biological systems are actually responsible for that effect?**

The most important PGx mechanisms involve:

- drug-metabolizing enzymes
- drug transporters
- drug targets
- immune-related genes
- interactions between genetics and other clinical factors

This module introduces the main biological players without becoming too technical too early.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain how PGx genes influence drug response biologically
- distinguish enzymes, transporters, and targets
- understand why some PGx effects involve prodrugs
- recognize that one drug may be influenced by more than one gene
- understand the concept of phenoconversion

---

## 1. From DNA to Drug Response

A genetic variant does not affect a drug directly.

Instead, it usually changes the function of something biological, such as:

- an enzyme
- a transporter
- a receptor
- a signaling protein
- an immune recognition molecule

This altered biological function may then change:

- drug concentration
- drug activation
- drug effect
- toxicity risk
- adverse reaction risk

### Simple model

A useful way to think about this is:

**DNA variant → altered biological function → altered drug response**

This same logic will reappear again and again throughout PGx.

---

## 2. The Main Classes of PGx Genes

For a beginner, the easiest way to organize PGx genes is into four groups.

### A. Drug-metabolizing enzymes
These genes influence how the body chemically processes a drug.

### B. Drug transporters
These genes influence how drugs move into, out of, or between tissues.

### C. Drug targets and response genes
These genes influence how strongly the body responds to the drug once it reaches its target.

### D. Immune-related genes
These genes influence whether the immune system reacts abnormally to a medication.

This classification is very useful because it connects biology to the clinical outcome.

## 3. Drug-Metabolizing Enzymes

Drug-metabolizing enzymes are among the most important PGx proteins.

They help chemically transform drugs in the body, often in the liver.

### Why they matter

If enzyme activity is reduced:
- the drug may remain in the body longer
- drug exposure may increase
- toxicity risk may increase

If enzyme activity is increased:
- the drug may be cleared more quickly
- drug exposure may decrease
- treatment may become less effective

### Important reminder

This is often true for **active drugs**, but the logic may reverse for **prodrugs**.

---

## 4. The CYP Enzymes

The most famous PGx enzyme family is the **cytochrome P450 (CYP)** family.

These enzymes are heavily involved in drug metabolism.

Some of the best-known PGx genes belong to this family, including:

- **CYP2D6**
- **CYP2C19**
- **CYP2C9**
- **CYP3A5**

You do not need to memorize all of them yet. At this stage, the important idea is:

### Key concept
Different CYP genes metabolize different drugs, and genetic variation in these genes can strongly affect treatment response.

### Beginner-friendly way to think about CYP enzymes
They are like biological processors that handle many drugs differently depending on the person’s genotype.

---

## 5. Active Drugs vs Prodrugs

This is one of the most important concepts in PGx.

### Active drug
An active drug works in the form in which it is given.

If metabolism is reduced, the active drug may remain in the body longer and possibly become more toxic.

### Prodrug
A prodrug must be converted by the body into its active form.

If activation is reduced, the treatment may fail because not enough active drug is produced.

### Why this matters

A learner often assumes:

**slower metabolism = stronger drug effect**

That is not always true.

For a prodrug, slower metabolism or reduced activation may actually mean:

**less active drug → less effect**

### Practical examples
- **Codeine** depends on conversion into morphine
- **Clopidogrel** depends on activation to become effective

---

## 6. Drug Transporters

Not all PGx effects come from metabolism.

Some genes affect **transporters**, which help move drugs across membranes.

Transporters may influence:

- absorption from the gut
- entry into tissues
- uptake into liver cells
- removal from cells
- elimination from the body

### Why transporters matter

A drug may reach:
- too little of the target tissue
- too much of a sensitive tissue
- different levels in blood than expected

So even if metabolism is normal, a transporter difference can still change the final outcome.

### Educational point

Enzymes change the chemical form of the drug.  
Transporters change where the drug goes.

That is a very important distinction.

---

## 7. Drug Targets and Response Genes

Some PGx genes affect the drug target itself or the body’s response to the drug.

Examples may include:

- receptors
- enzymes that are the target of the drug
- downstream signaling proteins
- pathway regulators

### Why these genes matter

Even if the drug concentration is normal, the response may still differ because:

- the drug binds differently
- the target behaves differently
- the pathway responds differently

This belongs more to **pharmacodynamics** than pharmacokinetics.

### Practical meaning

A patient may have:
- expected drug exposure
- but unexpectedly weak response
- or unexpectedly strong sensitivity

because the difference is in the **response system**, not in drug handling.

---

## 8. Immune-Related PGx

Some PGx effects are not mainly about metabolism or target binding.

Instead, they involve the immune system.

These effects are especially important in severe adverse drug reactions.

### Key idea

A genetic variant may increase the chance that the immune system reacts dangerously to a drug.

This can lead to:

- severe hypersensitivity
- serious skin reactions
- other major adverse responses

### Why this matters for the course

Later, in the HLA module, we will study these reactions in much more detail.

For now, the key point is:

PGx is not only about whether a drug works or not.  
It is also about whether a drug may be dangerous in a genetically susceptible person.

---

## 9. One Drug Can Be Influenced by More Than One Gene

A common beginner mistake is to think:

**one drug = one gene**

In reality, many drugs are influenced by more than one gene.

For example, a drug may be affected by:

- a metabolizing enzyme
- a transporter
- a target gene
- an immune-related gene

### Why this matters

Drug response is often a network problem, not a single-gene problem.

This is one reason PGx interpretation can become complex in real clinical settings.

---

## 10. Phenoconversion

This is one of the most important bridge concepts between basic biology and real clinical interpretation.

### What is phenoconversion?

Phenoconversion happens when a person’s actual drug-metabolizing behavior is different from what their genotype alone would predict.

### Why can that happen?

Because other factors can change the functional activity of a pathway, especially:

- co-medications
- strong enzyme inhibitors
- strong enzyme inducers
- disease state
- inflammation

### Example logic

A person may genetically look like a normal metabolizer, but because of another medication, they may behave more like a poor metabolizer.

### Why this is important

This means:

**genotype is not always the full phenotype**

That is why PGx always has to be interpreted together with clinical context.

## 11. Core PGx Genes to Know Early

At this stage, you do not need deep technical details. But it is useful to become familiar with a few names you will see repeatedly.

### Drug metabolism
- CYP2D6
- CYP2C19
- CYP2C9
- CYP3A5

### Other important PGx genes
- TPMT
- NUDT15
- DPYD
- UGT1A1
- SLCO1B1
- VKORC1

### For later immune-related PGx
- HLA genes

### Important note

At this stage, focus on recognizing the names and broad roles.

Detailed interpretation of these genes comes later.

## 12. A Simple Summary Table

| Gene type | Main role | Typical PGx consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Metabolizing enzyme | Changes the drug chemically | Alters exposure, activation, or clearance |
| Transporter | Moves the drug between compartments | Alters tissue access or elimination |
| Target/response gene | Changes drug effect at the site of action | Alters sensitivity or efficacy |
| Immune-related gene | Changes immune recognition of the drug | Alters hypersensitivity or severe reaction risk |

This table is a good mental shortcut for early learners.

## 14. Key Takeaways

- PGx genes influence drug response through biological mechanisms, not directly.
- The main PGx gene classes include enzymes, transporters, targets, and immune-related genes.
- CYP enzymes are among the most important PGx genes in drug metabolism.
- Active drugs and prodrugs behave differently in PGx.
- One drug can be influenced by more than one gene.
- Phenoconversion reminds us that genotype alone is not always enough to predict real-world drug response.
