# Module 1: Foundations of PGx

## 1. What Is PGx?

**Pharmacogenomics (PGx)** is the study of how genetic variation influences drug response.

It helps explain why two patients receiving the same drug at the same dose may experience very different outcomes, such as:

- good response
- poor response
- toxicity
- severe adverse reaction

PGx aims to support safer and more effective treatment by using genetic information to help guide drug and dose selection.

## 2. Pharmacogenetics vs Pharmacogenomics

These terms are often used interchangeably, but they can be distinguished conceptually:

- **Pharmacogenetics** often refers to the effect of one gene or a small number of genes on drug response.
- **Pharmacogenomics** is broader and may include multiple genes, pathways, genome-wide variation, and system-level interpretation.

In practice, many people use **PGx** as a general term for both.

## 3. Why PGx Matters

Drug response is not the same in every person. Differences may come from:

- genetics
- age
- liver function
- kidney function
- diet
- smoking
- drug-drug interactions
- disease state
- adherence

PGx focuses specifically on the **genetic component** of this variability.

## 4. Two Main Dimensions of PGx

### Pharmacokinetics
This asks:

**What does the body do to the drug?**

This includes:
- absorption
- distribution
- metabolism
- excretion

Many important PGx genes influence drug metabolism.

### Pharmacodynamics
This asks:

**What does the drug do to the body?**

This includes:
- drug targets
- receptors
- signaling pathways
- biological response
- immune recognition

Some PGx effects involve the drug target itself, while others involve immune-mediated reactions.

## 5. Main Types of PGx Effects

PGx effects are often grouped into three broad categories:

### A. Altered drug exposure
A person may metabolize or clear a drug too slowly or too quickly.

Possible result:
- too much drug exposure and toxicity
- too little drug exposure and treatment failure

### B. Altered drug activation
Some drugs are **prodrugs**, meaning they must be activated by the body.

If activation is impaired:
- the drug may not work well

If activation is too strong:
- active drug exposure may become excessive

### C. Increased risk of severe adverse reaction
Some genetic variants increase the risk of dangerous side effects, including immune-mediated hypersensitivity reactions.

## 6. Simple Examples

### Codeine
Codeine is a classic example of a **prodrug**.

It needs to be converted into morphine by the body. If this conversion is poor, the drug may not provide enough pain relief. If conversion is too strong, toxicity risk may increase.

### Clopidogrel
Clopidogrel is also a prodrug.

If activation is reduced, the drug may be less effective.

### Warfarin
Warfarin is influenced by more than one gene, showing that PGx is often not a simple one-gene problem.

## 7. PGx Is Not the Whole Story

PGx is important, but it does not explain everything.

A complete clinical interpretation may also need to consider:

- co-medications
- organ function
- inflammation
- indication
- age
- dose
- adherence
- other clinical variables

So PGx should be viewed as an important layer within a larger treatment decision framework.

## 8. Key Terms to Know

### Gene
A segment of DNA associated with a biological function.

### Variant
A difference in DNA sequence between individuals.

### Genotype
The genetic state observed at a variant or gene.

### Phenotype
The observable functional consequence, such as a metabolizer category.

### Prodrug
A drug that needs to be converted into an active form.

### Adverse drug reaction
An unwanted harmful effect caused by a drug.

## 9. Learning Goal of This Module

After completing this module, the learner should be able to:

- explain what PGx is
- distinguish pharmacokinetics from pharmacodynamics
- describe major categories of PGx effects
- understand why genetics can alter efficacy or toxicity
- recognize that PGx is only one part of clinical decision-making
