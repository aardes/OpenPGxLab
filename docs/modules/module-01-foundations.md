# Module 1: Foundations of PGx

## At a Glance

Pharmacogenomics (PGx) studies how genetic differences between people can influence the way they respond to medicines.

This matters because the **same drug at the same dose** may lead to very different outcomes in different patients. One person may improve, another may have little benefit, and another may develop toxicity or a serious adverse reaction.

In this module, we build the basic concepts needed for all the later modules.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain what pharmacogenomics is
- distinguish pharmacogenetics from pharmacogenomics
- understand the difference between pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics
- describe the main ways genetics can influence drug response
- recognize why PGx is useful, but not sufficient on its own, for clinical decision-making

---

## 1. What Is PGx?

**Pharmacogenomics (PGx)** is the study of how genetic variation influences drug response.

It helps explain why two patients receiving the same medication at the same dose may experience different outcomes, such as:

- good response
- poor response
- toxicity
- severe adverse reaction

The goal of PGx is to support **safer**, **more effective**, and sometimes **more personalized** treatment by using genetic information as one part of the clinical decision process.

### Simple way to think about it

PGx asks questions like:

- Does this person break down the drug too slowly?
- Does this person break down the drug too quickly?
- Does this person activate the drug properly?
- Does this person carry a genetic risk for a dangerous reaction?

---

## 2. Pharmacogenetics vs Pharmacogenomics

These two terms are often used interchangeably, but there is a useful conceptual distinction.

### Pharmacogenetics
Usually refers to the effect of **one gene** or a **small number of genes** on drug response.

### Pharmacogenomics
Usually refers to a **broader view**, including:
- multiple genes
- pathways
- genome-wide variation
- integrated interpretation

In practice, many people use **PGx** as a general term for both.

### Practical takeaway

For learners, it is enough to remember this:

- **pharmacogenetics** = often narrower  
- **pharmacogenomics** = often broader  

But in real-world discussions, the boundary is not always strict.

---

## 3. Why PGx Matters

Drug response is not the same in every person.

Two patients can take the same drug and still have very different results because of differences in:

- genetics
- age
- liver function
- kidney function
- diet
- smoking
- co-medications
- disease state
- adherence

PGx focuses specifically on the **genetic component** of this variability.

### Why this is clinically important

If we understand the relevant genetics, we may be able to:

- choose a better drug
- choose a safer dose
- reduce the risk of toxicity
- reduce trial-and-error prescribing
- better understand unexpected treatment failure

However, genetics is only **one part** of the full clinical picture.

---

## 4. Two Main Dimensions of PGx

One of the most important foundations in PGx is the distinction between:

- **pharmacokinetics**
- **pharmacodynamics**

### Pharmacokinetics (PK)

Pharmacokinetics asks:

**What does the body do to the drug?**

This includes:

- absorption
- distribution
- metabolism
- excretion

Many important PGx genes affect **drug metabolism**, especially enzymes involved in processing medicines in the liver and other tissues.

### Pharmacodynamics (PD)

Pharmacodynamics asks:

**What does the drug do to the body?**

This includes:

- drug targets
- receptors
- downstream signaling
- biological effect
- immune recognition

Some PGx effects are due to changes in how the drug is handled. Others are due to changes in how the body **responds** to the drug.

---

## 5. A Simple PK vs PD Summary

| Concept | Simple meaning |
|---|---|
| Pharmacokinetics | What the body does to the drug |
| Pharmacodynamics | What the drug does to the body |
| Typical PGx impact in PK | Drug is broken down too slowly or too quickly |
| Typical PGx impact in PD | Drug target or response pathway behaves differently |

## 6. Main Types of PGx Effects

PGx effects can be grouped into three broad categories.

### A. Altered drug exposure

A person may metabolize or clear a drug too slowly or too quickly.

Possible result:

- too much drug exposure → higher toxicity risk
- too little drug exposure → reduced effectiveness

### B. Altered drug activation

Some drugs are **prodrugs**, meaning they are not fully active when given. They must first be converted into an active form by the body.

Possible result:

- poor activation → drug may not work well
- excessive activation → active drug exposure may become too high

### C. Increased risk of severe adverse reaction

Some genetic variants increase the risk of serious side effects, including immune-mediated hypersensitivity reactions.

Possible result:

- dangerous toxicity
- severe skin reactions
- life-threatening adverse events in some settings

### Practical takeaway

PGx is not only about “fast” and “slow” metabolism.

It can affect:

- **how much drug is present**
- **whether the drug becomes active**
- **how the body responds to the drug**
- **whether the person is at higher risk of severe toxicity or hypersensitivity**

---

## 7. Simple Clinical Examples

At this stage, we do not need all the technical details. The goal is only to build intuition.

### Codeine

Codeine is a classic example of a **prodrug**.

It needs to be converted into morphine by the body. If this conversion is poor, the patient may get little pain relief. If conversion is too strong, the patient may be at higher risk of toxicity.

### Clopidogrel

Clopidogrel is also a prodrug.

If its activation is reduced, the drug may be less effective.

### Warfarin

Warfarin is influenced by more than one gene.

This is a useful reminder that PGx is often **not** a simple one-gene problem.

### Educational point

These examples show that PGx can affect drugs in different ways:

- by changing metabolism
- by changing activation
- by changing sensitivity or dose requirement

---

## 8. PGx Is Not the Whole Story

PGx is important, but it does not explain everything.

A complete clinical interpretation may also need to consider:

- co-medications
- organ function
- inflammation
- disease context
- age
- dose
- route of administration
- adherence
- other clinical variables

### Why this matters

A person may have a genotype that suggests one type of response, but in real life the final outcome may still be influenced by:

- another drug
- impaired liver function
- poor adherence
- dose differences
- acute illness

So PGx should be viewed as a **powerful layer of information**, not as a complete answer by itself.

## 9. Key Terms to Know

### Gene
A segment of DNA associated with a biological function.

### Variant
A difference in DNA sequence between individuals.

### Genotype
The genetic state observed at a variant or gene.

### Phenotype
The observable or inferred functional consequence of a genotype, such as a metabolizer category.

### Prodrug
A drug that needs to be converted into an active form.

### Adverse drug reaction
An unwanted or harmful effect caused by a drug.

### Toxicity
Harm caused by excessive drug effect or exposure.

### Hypersensitivity
An abnormal or excessive immune-mediated reaction to a drug.

## 10. A Simple Conceptual Model

A beginner-friendly way to think about PGx is:

**Genetic variation → altered protein function → altered drug response**

This altered response may appear as:

- reduced efficacy
- increased toxicity
- dose sensitivity
- increased risk of adverse reaction

## 12. Key Takeaways

- PGx studies how genetic variation influences drug response.
- Different patients can respond differently to the same drug and dose.
- PGx includes both **pharmacokinetic** and **pharmacodynamic** effects.
- Genetics can influence drug exposure, drug activation, efficacy, toxicity, and adverse reaction risk.
- Some drugs are **prodrugs** and must be activated by the body.
- PGx is important, but it is only one part of clinical decision-making.
